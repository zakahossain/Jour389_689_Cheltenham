Baltimore's Oldest in Service
The Rev. Mr. Bragg claimed the
honor of being the oldest min-
ister in Baltimore, in point of
service.
He has been in the min-
istry fifty-two years, having been
ordained in 1888, the twelfth
Episcopal clergyman of his race.
He was considered an authority
on the history of the colored
church and on the history of col-
ored Americans, Marylanders in
particular. One of his best-known
works is his "Men of Maryland."
which is considered authoritative.
Educated in Virginia
He was born in Warrenton.
N.C., January 25, 1863, the son
of George F. and Mary Bragg.
who soon after his birth moved
to Petersburg, Va.
He studied first at the Episco-
pal Parochial School, then in the
St. Stephen Normal School and
in the Bishop Payne Divinity
School, all in Petersburg.
His education was supplement-
ed by private instruction from a
master of languages, under whom
he studied Latin, Greek. Hebrew
and philosophy. In 1881, he was
appointed a page in the Virginia
Legislature and a little later was
promoted to the postmastership
of the House of Delegates.
Pioneer Publisher
In 1882 when just 18 years old.
he founded and edited the Vir-
ginia Lancet, one of the first col-
ored weeklies published in Vir-
ginia. He conducted this news-
paper for five years and on Janu-
агу 12, 1887, in St. Stephen
Church, Petersburg, he was or-
dained deacon by Bishop Whittle
of Virginia.
He immediately left for Nor-
folk. where he began his min-
istry as head of a small Episco-
pal mission. He remained there
for nearly five years, during
which time he formally organized
Grace Church, secured the lot.
built a new church and rectory
and improved the cld school
building.
He also carried on a day indus-
trial school in connection with
his work.
Was Hampton Trustee
During this time, Governor
Fitzhugh Lee of Virginia appoint-
ed him one of the State's trustees
of the Hampton Institute, where
he served for four years.
In December 1888, he was ad-
vanced to the priesthood by Bish-
op Whittle in St. Luke Church,
Norfolk, and in the fall of 1891,
he accepted the call to come to
St. James Church, Baltimore.
which at that time was located
on High Street in East Baltimore
and had a congregation "ready to
die."
Growth Steady
The Rev. Mr. Bragg set to work,
increased the communicant list
from 63 to over 200, and erected
a new church building at Park
Avenue and Preston Street, in
1901.
!
In 1931. the church had prog-
ressed to the point where it had
over 300 members and was:
able to give nearly $1,000 a year
to charities, missions and bene-
volence.
In 1932, the old St. James prop-
erty on Park Avenue was pur-
chased by the city and the church
moved into the edifice formerly
occupied by the white Church of
Ascension. at Arlington and
Lafayette Avenues and has re-
mained there ever since.
Founded Friendless Home
Soon after coming to Baiti-
more in 1899, the Rev. Mr. Bragg
founded the Maryland Home for
Friendless Colored Children,
which is now known as the
Friendless Home for Colored
Boys and is located in Catons-
ville.
For three decades, the Rev. Mr.
Bragg was secretary of the An-
nual Conference of the Episcopal
Church Workers, and edited the
Ledger. which afterward merged
