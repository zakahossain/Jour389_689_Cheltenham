400 at Urban
League Dinner
Honor Lewis
BALTIMORE
More than 400 persons of both
races joined in paying tribute to
Edward S. Lewis at the Baltimore
Urban League's twelfth annual
dinner meeting Thursday evening
at the Cadoa, 118 W. Franklin
Street.
Mr. Lewis was executive secre-
tary of the local branch for eleven
year, leaving the first of the
month for a similar post in New
York.

Optimism Expressed
The participating speakers all
struck notes of optimism in re-
counting the gains for workers ob-
tained by the league during
1942, which augur even more
work opportunities in the future.
Before reviewing his administra-
tion, Mr. Lewis asked the guests
to join with him in a silent tri-
bute to the late Father John T.
Gillard, a board member, who
made it possible for the dinner to
be held in the Cadoa several
years ago after Johns Hopkins had
refused to open its doors:
Credits Staff, Board
He also paid special tribute to
the members of his staff and
board and announced that Dr.
Bruce McDonald, white, president
of the board, had agreed to serve
another year.
Mr. Lewis cited the increase in
the league's annual budget, dur-
ing the past eleven years, from
$4,000 to a proposed $14,000 for
1943, and said there still remain
the following problems to be
solved:
(1) department store discrim-
ination, (2) uniforms for colored
police, and (3) equalization of
school facilities which, he esti-
mated will cost a million dollars.
His insistence that a colored
person's appointment to the
school board would help solve
the latter problem brought sus-
tained applause
Not Discouraged
The New York secretary said,
however, that he is not discour-
aged by the total picture - not
even by the FEPC reversals.
"We gathered here tonight to
represent one of the elements of
a new world society not based on
race, but on opportunities, democ-
racy, and freedom," he said in
closing.
"I believe that this really is a
people's war and that the gates
of hell shall not prevail against
the new day now dawning."
Allen Sees New Hope
A. J. Allen, league industrial
secretary, in his annual report, de-
clared that the most significant
factor in the opening of new em-
ployment opportunities for col-
ored workers is that their chil-
dren, likewise, have a reasonable
hope for future employment.
He revealed that league efforts
have made $750,000 in earnings
available to members of the build-
ing trades heretofore denied em-
ployment.
Hepbron Praises Lewis
James M. Hepbron, white di-
rector of the Criminal Justice
Commission and the Community
Fund, paid a personal tribute to
Mr. Lewis, whom he termed his
"friend, counsellor and co-
worker.".
He discussed the work of the
Governor's Commission on Juve-
nile Delinquency and indicated
that its recommendations would
be made quite soon.
J. Harvey Kerns of Cincinnati,
who succeeds Mr. Lewis as ex-
ecutive secretary, was present. He
will take up his duties March 1.
Mr. and Mrs. Lewis received
gifts from the Social Workers'
Roundtable,
the Book-of-the-
Month Club, the Painters' Asso-
ciation and the Urban League
Women's Auxiliary.
Several musical numbers were
rendered by the Baltimore Sing-
ers, directed by Gerald B. Wilson.
Among those present were Congress-
man Daniel Ellison; Walter Sondheim,
director of U.S. Employment Service in
Maryland; Kenneth Douty, War Man-
power Commission assistant State direc-
tor; Mr. and Mrs. Vance Thomas of
Cheltenham School for Boys;
G. Laird Ferguson, and Saul Purdue,
Glenn L. Martin Company's Canton Di-
vision; Harry Cohen, Baltimore Federa-
tion of Labor: James Drury, post agent,
National Maritime Union;
Councilman Jacob Edelman, Bert
Hanner, WFBR program manager; Sid-
ney Katz, Md. CIO Industrial Council;
Harold Buchman, local attorney; and
Max Orlov, painters' union representa-
tive.
Bob McGuinn, lawyer for the carpen-
ters' union, which now has more than
500 members; Dallas Nicholas, presi-
dent, Monumental Bar Association; Mrs.
Violet Hill Whyte, policewoman.
