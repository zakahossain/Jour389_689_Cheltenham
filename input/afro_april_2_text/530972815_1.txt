IN AND ABOUT
BALTIMORE
By K. W.
THE NEW STREET
Coming to this office Friday after-
noon two young women hailed me at
Druid Hill Avenue and St. Mary's
Street and blandly asked me where
was Jazzbo Street. At first I thought
they had discovered I was from the
country and were out to have some
fun, but suddenly I thought of Jas-
per Street, but a square above. I
told them, and that was IT.

WHAT'S THE NUMBER?
It was told in Tuesday's edition
of the AFRO of Norfolk's num-
bers determination from prices dis-
played in provision stores, but the
Virginia City has nothing on Balti-
more when it comes to the playing
of lotteries in the Jewish neighbor-
hood stores of the Northwestern sec-
tion.
These merchants collect thousands
of dollars weekly from players and
pay the bets to the winners. There
is no attempt at concealment, for I
went into two last week in which I
had never been. The wagers were
made in rear rooms, but a general
conversation was carried on in the
stores before me whom they had
never seen. I daresay mey have no
fear of the law or assume all co'
ed people play the digits.
In one of the stores, at the time
I was there, was a small boy who
came to purchase a yo-yo, and to
receive free the ground-work for a
pre-numbers education which will
admit him to the gambling colleges
where craps and cards will lead to
his post-graduate course in the
higher institutions, Cheltenham, the
jail. House of Correction, and peni-
tentiary. Jews are the colored peo-
ple's best friends? Oh yeah?

DON'T BE FUNNY
*
My Nordic fellow columnist and
brother Baltimorean, Carroll Du-
laney, has evidently imbibed of the
draught of prejudice and bias which
makes him throw all regard to de-
cency and usual sense of fair play
into Bodkin Creek. Here's how he
feels about it:
"I have seen only one intelli-
gent white man die at the end of
a rope by order of the court, and
I believe it was the humiliation
of the thing which hurt him
most.
"The others whose sudden, but
not unexpected, departure from
this world I had the questionable
honor to witness were dumb,
brutish human beings. And all
of them, white and black, seemed
deeply sensible of the fact that
they were the heroes of the oc-
casion.
"Certainly, of the negroes, not
cne would have exchanged places
with the Sheriff. They had been
worked up to a high pitch of re-
ligious emotion, and all were sure
of a safe landing in some prom-
ised land where work was un-
known, and life was
grand
round of gin, and chicken din-
ners.
a
"I am convinced that each and
every one of them choked a final
prayer of thanksgiving for a civi-
lization that warrants such a
taking off."
So there you are. The same dam-
nable concept, but the difference is
that it is spread by a man who
knows better. He is aware that ALL
colored people do not drink gin,
shoot craps, and, I'll add, do the
Charleston.
In pre-Volstead days, who con-
sumed the gin rickeys, and gin bucks
at the Southern, Rennert, Carroll-,
ton, Stafford, and Belvedere? Are
the bottles that the trash men haul
from the rear of houses inhabited by
whites, marked GIN, property of
the butlers and the maids of today?
Who woos Dame Fortune at Ben-
ny Franklin's and via craps at John
Pat McGovern's? For whom did the
old-time chefs prepare fried chicken
in the restaurants of Baltimore with
such excellence that the fame of the
dish is known around the world?
The doomed men may have "choked
a final prayer of thanksgiving," but
it was for deliverance from the com-
mercial wisecracks of Carroll Du-
laney.
