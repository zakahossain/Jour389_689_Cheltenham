War Training Week?
Separate, Unequal
Jobs at Cheltenham
Collins a Pioneer
By B. M. PHILLIPS
This is War Training Week.
Emphasis is on increasing enroll-
ment in the city's war vocational
training schools to prepare for
vital jobs in war industries.
The campaign is sponsored
by the vocational war produc-
tion training division of the
city's schools, and it ought to be
conducted without regard to
race, color or creed.
But it isn't. Advertisements
on streetcars and trolleys through-
out the city urge trainees to en-
roll at Poly and Boys' Vocational
Schools, both white.
Ads Seek Whites Only
Radio ads beg women to sign
up at the Howard Street Re-
cruiting Center, "white."
Nowhere is mention made
of similar training at Schools
No. 453 at 775 Waesche Street;
No. 456. 1315 Druid Hill Av-
caue, or Carver Vocational No.
454 at Lajayette and Carroll-
ton Avenues.
The average person would
presume, therefore, that Bal-
timore was a democratic city
and that training schools plug-
ged over the air and on pub-
lic vehicles were open to all.
But Baltimore has a dual school
ystem which is supposed to pro-
ide equal and equitable facilities,
which of course it does not.
Nevertheless, under this dual
system, if training classes for
whites are advertised during a
city-wide campaign, those for col-
ored should likewise be pushed.
James O. Proctor, supervisor of
training, indicated, Thursday,
that absence of radio advertising
for colored schools was an over-
sight and promised to do bet-
ter. You can't win a war with mis-
takes and promises.
To put over the program of
advertising, the colored school
principals solicited funds from
merchants to sponsor paid ad-
vertising in colored newspa-
pers.
A vocational fund used to print
advertising signs for white
schools was not available to them.
Symptom of Fascism
This matter of promotion of a
special drive in war vocational
schools may seem a small matter
on the surface, but it is a part of
the vicious circle which has sur-
rounded the war training pro-
gram in our schools for three
years.
If this war continues to get i
tougher, the Baltimore school
officials will curse the day they
allowed the color of a man to
interfere with the war effort.
Wary of Cheltenham Jobs
There are twenty job openings
at the Cheltenham School for
Boys. Officials are unhappy that
applicants are few.
I'm not surprised. Cheltenham
has long been a hell-hole and col-
ored people have been buffeted
about too much. They want to be
sure they'll get a square deal and
no segregation.
Securing people to fill jobs
at institutions of this sort is,
after all, the job of the super-
intendent. If Cheltenham has at
its head someone who is inter-
ested in filling those jobs, then
it will be done in short order.
USO Cleans House
The Gold Street USO Center
says its 450 junior hostesses have
undergone special training to pro-
vide clean. interesting entertain-
ment for men who visit the cen-
ter.
In the past month, a few girls
who have been branded as "un-
desirable" by fellow hostesses
have been eliminated. The USO
invites as hostesses only young
women who have high standards.
says Mrs. Marian Banfield, assist-
ant program director in charge
of girls' activities.
