CHELTENHAM, Md. Rigid si-
lence on the part of officials at the
boys' reformatory here causes
mystery to surround the shooting
of Aubrey Bronson, 19, by a white
guard named John Hurley, Sat-
urday.
Barefooted inmates of the re-
formatory would reveal no facts
concerning the gun play, and visi-
tors are permitted to go about the
grounds with understanding that
they will not question any of the
boys.
The state's attorney, Allen
Bowie, claims to have made a per-
sonal investigation of the matter,
and has released the guard from
arrest on a bond of $1,000.
Mr. Bowie's Version
Mr. Bowie's version of the
shooting is:
"A bunch of the boys were go-
ing to be fed and he (Bronson) re-
fused to get in line.
"Later, Bronson went into a
room where the guard (Hurley)
was sitting and asked for a note
of some kind about the matter.
In a resulting argument, the guard
attempted to strike him (Bron-
son) with a cane.
"Other boys started to the aid
of Bronson and a guard named
Grier came to separate Bronson

and Hurley, who were struggling
for possession of the stick.
"In a following scuffle, a bullet
was fired from Hurley's gun and
Bronson was hit, but I don't know
where."
Further questioning of the
attorney revealed that what had
been called a cane was really a
small sized club.
Mr. Bowie, according to John
B. Pyles, superintendent, has left
instructions for the authorities of
the reform school to keep their
mouths shut about the matter.
Conditions Getting Worse
In the meanwhile, conditions
are rapidly going down at the in-
stitution, according to the super-
intendent, and Saturday's shooting
has not helped matters much.
Mr. Pyles stated that the ap-
pearance of AFRO news stories
about the conditions of the insti-
tution has helped to foster unrest.
All inmates have been instruct-
ed to keep their eyes on the
ground when any of the women
ascend steps on the grounds.
These women are said to include
the wife of Superintendent Pile.
Miss Hattie M., his secretary, and
a third who has charge of the din-
ing room.
Punishment of the boys who
fail to keep their eyes lowered is
set at fifty lashes on their bare
backs.
Supt. Pyles denied having any
knowledge of whippings adminis-
tered to the boys, who are inmates,
and displayed anger when ques-
tioned about a rule which pro-
hibits boys from raising their eyes
when women are going up the
steps of the institution.
"I am trying to make them well
mannered," he said, "and I tell
them to turn their backs when
women up going up stairs because
it is impolite to look."
Rule Necessary
When told that his idea of good
manners was not practiced any-
where, and persons coming up the
steps behind a woman are not
necessarily paying attention to
her, Mr. Pyles admitted that such
was the case but he said that the
rule was necessary at Chelten-
ham.
He also stated that the visiting
hours at the institution have been
limited because of frequent es-
capes.
Mr. Pyles stated that four boys
escaped last Sunday and have not
been apprehended.
Autoist Gives Ald
One boy, according to Mr. Pyles,
was picked up in front of the ad-
ministration office by an autoist.
The superintendent admitted
that the guards and boys have
been having frequent clashes of
late, and he also declared that the
trouble between Bronson and
Hurley might have been brewing
for a long time.
Two Guards Hurt
- Two guards were seriously in-
jured by those boys no longer than
the other day," said Mr. Pyles.
"What was the nature of their
injuries?" he was asked.
"Yes, sir, they were some se-
rious," he returned and started to
change the subject.
"Can you describe those injur-
ies?" he was asked.
"Well, one of the guards was
cut about the face in a close-quar-
ter fight." he stated slowly.
He failed to find a way to de-
scribe how the other guard was
hurt.
Version of Relatives
The version of the shooting
given by the relatives is:
"Bronson was shot by a guard
who was yelling at a group of
boys and thought that the victim
said something 'smart.'
"The guard had attempted to
strike Bronson with a club, but
the youth succeeded in wresting
the cudgel from his assailant.
"Another guard seized the man
in an attempt to stop him from
hurting Bronson, but the latter
drew a gun and shot over the
shoulder of the would-be peace-
maker.
"No official notification was
given of the shooting, although
Bronson was sent to the hospital
and it was known that he was
suffering from a punctured lung."
Office Hadn't Heard
The Baltimore office of the
House of Reformation, queried by
the AFRO on Tuesday morning,
had had no report of the shoot-
ing and learned of it for the first
time from the AFRO.
Cheltenham is a private school
to which the city and state send
male delinquents under 21. Near-
ly every Baltimore grand jury
since 1918 has condemned the
place as unfit and unprepared to
give correctional training or make
good citizens of the boys who are
sent there.
State Scored
The state has also been scored
for sending colored boys to a pri-
vate school, conducted and oper-
ated by white persons who have
only monetary interest in the
work, while white boys are sent
to well-manned reform schools
operated by the state.
Cheltenham guards are said to
be jittery since three boys escaped
from the institution Sunday be-
fore last, and four more ran away
last Sunday.
In one case, a visiting woman
is said to have helped the boys
flee by hiding them in her car.
She was caught at the Hanover
Street bridge, Baltimore, and
forced to carry them back to the
school.
So many boys are escaping that
Sunday one visitor was told that
visiting hours will be restricted to
the second Sunday in the month.
Visitors Sunday were not per-
mitted to walk about the grounds
with the boys as usual. Privi-
leges were curtailed and boys saw
their visitors in the hallway near
the superintendent's office.
Wear White Coats
Boys in white coats and white
aprons were pointed out to visi-
tors Sunday as special waiters at
the tables of the white employees,
which included three white wom-
en. The boys are barefooted so
that they will make no noise at
meals.
