JURY RECOMMENDS STATE CONTROL AT CHELTENHAM
CHANGES URGED
AT BOYS' HOME
Retiring Grand Jury Recom-
mend That State Take Over
House of Reformation
SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT
Grand Juror Evans Sets
Forth Conditions and
Urges Sweeping Changes
That conditions at the Maryland
House of Reformation at Chelten-
ham are still unspeakably bad and
that a few changes have been made
since the last investigation by the
Grand Jury is set forth in a lengthyl
statement by Joseph P. Evans in a
report to that body.
This supplimental report, which
is the first of its kind to be pub-
lished in full in an official report
of the Grand Jury, not only seath-
ingly sets forth the lack of proper
administration of this institution,
but recommends sweeping changes
After stating that he found some
of the buildings in very unsanitary
condition, dirty bedding, he con-
tinues:
We next visited the Hospital,
where we found four sick boys,
two with pneumonia, one of these
boys looked so flushed in the face,
I felt his forehead, and the mat-
ter was hot fever much over a hun-
dred. Both boys had water bags
filled with simply well water, not
ice water, and each one of these
sick boys was laying there fight-
ing the fever with their bare hands,
not even a fan to keep the flies
out of their faces, not even a
screen in a window, or a door
screen. If they should happen to
fall off to sleep, in a minute their
mouths would be full of the
deadly germ breeding flies.
Ragged And Dirty
"We call your attention to the
care of these boys.
I only saw
two boys that had any kind of dif-
ferent clothes, all the others were
ragged and dirty, hair long and
frightful or combed, faces dirty and
clothing torn. Some of the small-
er boys legs and feet were cov-
ered with dirty rags tied around
them.
"The State and city would, no
doubt, reap a greater reward from
the money and energy that is be-
ing spent each year, with practi-
cal and beneficial results, except to
give a few white men jobs to
guard and drive the boys around,
much who are not fit for teach-
ing for these poor unfortunate
boys other than their salaries.
Negro Teachers Asked
"I cannot see any good and suffi-
cient reason why colored teachers
are not employed in Cheltenham,
except there is a blot placed
upon our law records by the legis-
lature of this State that says that
there shall be no mixed schools
allowed in this State.
And with these statistics complied with, you would
have teachers, whose hearts were
in their work, taking the same
pride in these poor boys, as they
do throughout the whole State.
Colored teachers would not only
teach these boys but would see to
it that they would be kept clean,
their hair combed and clothes looked
after, and taught to do right.
Urges Self-Support
"If we were to add just half the
amount of wages those boys ought
to receive for their labor, the
institution could be wiped out. This
could easily be done by properly
handling of these boys in the
institution. They should
not only learn farming, but should
be taught to make bricks, lay
cement, carpentering and painting.
You could get colored men who
would efficiently train these boys
to do everything needed to be done
at this institution and at a great
saving and profit to the
State.
Would Pension Capt. Pyles
"I also wish
to compliment
Capt. Pyles, whom I have known
for over thirty years, for the great
work he did when he was in good
health. But now he has not only
lost one of his limbs, but is in
poor health otherwise.
"This worthy man should be
pensioned and sent to some rest
resort where he might regain his
health, because he is not physi-
cally able to properly look after all
of the many details necessary to
keep such a great institution, with
a large farm, and other things
connected with it, going."
The report also recommended a
home for feeble minded children,
colored matrons to care for the
quarters of the boys, a longer term
of schooling and the abolition of
the custom of farming out the boys
to outside employers.
Chief, however, among the rec-
ommendations of the general com-
mittee sent out by the Grand Jury
to make recommendations is that
urging the State to take over the
institution, "so that it does not de-
stroy the purpose for which boys
are committed to it."
