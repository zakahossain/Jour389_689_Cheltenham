Track Greats, New and Old,
Headline Penn Carnival
By GEORGE LYLE, JR.
SATURDAY.-Weather doubt-
ful, track heavy, high winds
sweeping across the track.
The faithful devotees of the
cinderpath are up betimes this
morning to see the running of the
forty-sixth annual Pennsylvania
Relay Carnival, the No. 1 sports
event of the day, which gets off
to a flying start with the heats
in the collegiate half-mile relay
championship.
North Texas Teachers win and
there is joy in the stands. Then
NYU, with George Hagans doing
a fine job, takes a first. Xavier,
turning in the best time in the
trials, easily takes Rutgers and
Columbia.
In Full Regalia
In the finals it appears that
the Nordic quartets have been
scared by the dark menace and
Texas wins, with Pitt and Xavier
elcse behind. Up in the stards,
a lanky Texan, completely garbed
in ten-gallon hat and cowboy
boots (minus spurs) attracts
plenty of attention.
Lunches are being brought
out of bags and boxes now
and the hotdog man gets lots
of takers as the clock reg-
1sters 11:30 and the next re-
lay is still an hour away.
Walt Arrington, Michigan State
field man, has just completed a
try in the broad jump, spanning
23 feet 11 5-8 inches to top the
field. His triumph is short-
lived though. Don Blount, white,
of Dartmouth, nosed him out with
a leap that carried him a frac-
tion over an inch farther.
Photographers appear from ev-
erywhere and began getting ac-
tion shots of the winner and sec-
ond-place man. Nice racket
that. Lock at Marshall Wilson,
lining up that trio of smartly
turned-out women. Wonder
whether he got their phone num-
bers? I'll ask him when he
comes over.

Threadgill on Hand
Who's that over there in front
of the high jump pit. Al Thread-
gill, guess he's thinking of days
when he was the big shot in that
event. One thing, he won't miss
any of the boys' form today; his
seat is almost on top of the
standards.
Goodness, the place is begin-
ning to show signs of life; there
come some of the high schcol
runners out for a little warming
up work. People are beginning
to come in, too.
One thing about this affair
it is really colorful, literally and
figuratively. Lock around in the
stands. These girls are really
coming on with their fine reds,
blues, yellows and stuff.
Out on the field, which inci-
dentally is a beautiful green car-
pet without a mark on it, the
radiant-hued sweat suits vie with
the colors in the stands.
Near tragedy: two girls are
conversing when suddenly
one turns to go back down
the ramp. The wind blow-
ing at just the right minute,
blows the second girl's veil
so that it is caught in the de-
parter's coat,
A scream, "Wait Camilla!",
averts more serious damage and
the day is safe.
What's this? the races have
started. Who's that boy? Har-
ry Dickens, from Cheltenham;
want to watch him in the next
few years he's got possibilities.
Ho, hum. Norcum High from
Portsmouth is going through its
usual routine. First at the 100-
yard mark and steadily retro-
gressing from there in. Too bad,
wish I could help that anchor
man close up about half that gap
anyway.

Great Finish
Talk about opportunity, Cam-
eraman Joe Sewell, of Washing-
ton, just missed a swell shot at
the finish of the Tuskegee event.
Uly Sams, ace anchor man, ran
his heart out to close up a twen-
ty-yard gap and, in his desperate
lunge at the tape, fell a heart-
beat behind Doan of Wayne Uni-
versity to cop second-place
honors.
Wonder why some athletes
distort their faces as if in
agony while others show no
more expression than the
well-known cow.
There's Tarzan Cooper, star of
the Renaissance five, seated just
behind Threadgill. And there
is Dick Moultrie, ex-captain of
the Lincoln team, who put on
many a fine show for the Frank-
lin Field goers during his stay at
the Lions' lair.
Juan Luyanda of Lincoln is
warming up for the high jump.
He's a Puerto Rican and is the
South American AAU titleholder.
Walt Arrington and Joshua
(Josh) Williamson join him.
