The State of Maryland and the
City of Baltimore were called for
a showdown at the Cheltenham
Reform school in the report of
the grand jury filed last week.
A special committee appointed
to investigate the conditions there
in their recommendations say in
part that the state should either ap-
propriate funds sufficient for the car-
rying on of the institution and then
see to it that it is properly done or
else withdraw all support and estab-
lish a State school which will not
be operated by private interests.
A lack of keen and intelligent in-
terest on the part of the city and
Hi state, inefficient management and
absolute methods in educational and
f-institutional work are given as the
short comings of the home at the
present.
No Justification
The committee in referring to the
practice of hiring boys out say:
The by-laws, rules and regulations
of your institutions stress the value
ON of sound, practical education, school
instruction being esteemed as one
of the chief means of reclamation.
Since our entry into the World
War, this part of the work of the
Institution has been lost sight of.
VO while a pernicious system of hiring
out the inmates to farmers in Prince;
George's and neighborhood counties
has been enforced. The alleged justi-
fication for this is that it was neces-
sary to help out the farmers during
the war. because of the lack of labor.
while from that time on the claim
is that the ncessities of that insti-
tution have been such that the en-
forced labor of these boys, hired out
to the farmers at 50c and 60c a day
in direct competition with higher paid
free labor has continued up to the
present time.
To Discontinue
This committee is thankful to state
that it is informed by the Superm-
tendent and by the Secretary of the
Board of Mangers that after ten years
this most objectionable method of
benefiting the farmers by furnishing
them with cheap Negro labor will
cease absolutely on September 15.
1927.
These officers have admitted
that in very many of these cases
the boys let out under this system
of practical peonage have not receiv-
need proper food or lodging and that
some of them have been mal-treated.
It is a vicious system and should nev-
er have been permitted to exist.
All Work
Of the 275 boys at the institution
on Novmber 30. 1926, 121 are listed
as general outside workers (presum-
ably those farmed out and over whom
the institution could have no real
oversight): 4 were employed in the
broom factory under contract with
an outside manufacturer: 40
were p
working on farms: 10 were emploved
as in the shoe and tailoring shops; 12 in
the laundries, and the remainder were
utilized in the general work of the
institution.
Apparently no industrial
training of any value is provided and
a boy, on leaving the institution. is
almost or quite as unfitted to face
the world as he was when commit-
ted.
No Education At All
The broom work is done by ma-
chinery, every boy doing only a small
part of the complete operation, and
the work is crude.
The boys, who
have been farmed out. have received
practically no education and those
remaining at the institution have re-
ceived no more.
Our opinion. from what we were
able to see. is that there is no edu-
cational system whatever. but that
the teaching of even the smallest
Obous is irregular, desultory and non-
productive.
We feel that the great
opportunity afforded this institution
for the reformation and training of
the boys has been neglected.

The institution is a private corpo-

members.
Since 1922. the state has
appropriated annually for the sun-
port of the institution $15.000. while
Baltimore City's annual contribution
has ranged from $30.000 to $37.000.
For the year ending November 30,
1926. the total receipts. including the
above appropriations, were $08.797.51. of

67.718 from the broom factory, $7.438

Should Withdraw Support
This committee feels strongly that
the state and city should support
this work adequately, seeing at the
same time that it is efficiently con-
ducted.
If they are unwilling to do
so while the institution is in pri-
vate hands, they should establish a
proner state reformatory for colored
boys and finance it properly with-
drawing entirely fom the Chelten-
ham institution the half-hearted and
insufficient support.
