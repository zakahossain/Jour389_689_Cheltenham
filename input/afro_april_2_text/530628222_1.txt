CHELTENHAM IN

CALLED FIRE TRAP
Hiring Out Of Boys Only
Another Kind Of Peon-
age
In a minority report filed
with the annual report of
the Grand Jury resulting
from their investigations
of city and state institu-
tions, T. Wallis Lansey,
manager of Druid Laundry
severely scored existing
conditions at the Reform
School for Colored youth at
Cheltenham, and offered
several suggestions for the
betterment of the institu-
tion.
In his report Mr. Lansey suggests
ine abolishment of the broom fau-
tory, where a large majority of the
boys are employed. Very few boys
after being dismissed from the in-
stitution are able to make a living
at this trade, he points out. Money
expended in this direction should
be used to improve the shoe mak-
ing and tailoring shops with compe-
tent instructors. If these trades
are properly taught it will not only
enable the boys to make their own
clothes and shoes, thus reducing the
expense to the state, but would
prepare them to make a living and
become useful citizens after their
dismissal, he declares.
Broom Factory Firetrap
-.
The broom factory is a veritable
firetrap, according to Mr. Lansey.
There is but one exit which is very
small, and due to the highly in-
flamable materials used in the mak-
ing of brooms, would result in many
deaths before the score or more of
boys employed there could make
their escape. As long as the broom
factory remains, he suggests the
building of another stairway lead-
ing from the basement where
factory is located.
Bread Soggy
the
While the bakery was clean and
sanitary, the bread made there was
neither wholesome or digestible, he
states. A loaf handed to him as
he passed through was heavy and
soggy. The buildings were well
kept. Of the 285 boys only six
were employed in the shoe shop at
the time of the visit; there was no
evidence that the trade was being
taught. The equipment was old,
scant, and antiquated. Neither
were there any signs of instruction
in the tailoring shop. The only in-
struments were six old sewing ma-
chines that revealed little evidence
of being used.
Calls Farm System Peonage
The system of hiring out the
boys to neighboring farmers as used
by the superintendent is described
as being but one step from slavery.
The boys get but from $25 to $40
a year for their services it is said.
This is collected by the institution
and saved until they are dismissed
at the age of 21.
ex-
The management complained of
the lack of funds to put the insti-
tution on a higher plane, Mr. Lan-
cey states, if the boys now hired
out to private farmers were em-
ployed by the institution, to farm
the 754 acres on which it is situ-
ated between class hours, they
would not only be able to defray
a large amount of their own
pense but would also be able to
hire competent instructors. An as-
sistant, who is a specialist in insti-
tutional work should be employed
who by scientific methods of farm-
ing could obtain the desired result.
Abolish the Aze Limit
The present practice of commit-
ting a boy to the institution until
he is 21 years of age should be
abolished, Mr. Lancey declares, and
the judges be empowered to limit
the boy's stay in accordance with
the misdemeanor committed. Many
boys after a year in the reform
school realize their error and would
come out better citizens. To com-
mit a boy of 12 who has made a
mistake to such an institution,
which does not better his condition,
until he reaches his majority is the
highest form of injustice.
Under present conditions if a boy
makes the ten grades through good
behavior he is subject to parole.
This is optional with th authori-
ties, however, and results in very
few boys being 'freed. This power
should be taken out of the hands
of the management and embodied
in the judges imposing the sentence,
he adds.
