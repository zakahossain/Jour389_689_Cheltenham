Marcus Garvey, head of the Universal Negro Im-5
provement Association and the Black Star Steamship
e, is free again.
Last week he walked out of the Tombs prison pend-
ing the hearing of his appeal in a higher United States
court. Three months he has been imprisoned, part of
his sentence of five years in Atlanta and $1,000 for using
the mails to defraud
P
r
Before his arrest, there was not in America a more
skillful financier or a more eloquent one. He had more
ways of taking in people's money and holding it than
wise old New York ever heard of. Banks, steamship
lines, laundries, chain-stores, funds of many kinds and r
classes were used by Mr. Garvey to gather in the "bits
of silver." Halted in one line, he turned quickly to
another, until the long arm of the law reached out for
a od stay.
The strange part about it is that Mr. Garvey's moral
sense does not allow him to admit that he is in error.
His arrest, he claims. is due to the hostility of enemies or
rival associations. His conviction came as the result of
"spite-work," he is a "martyr."
Maybe a term in Atlanta will change Mr. Garvey's
ideas about collecting money illegally, but certainly
three months in the Tombs has not.
