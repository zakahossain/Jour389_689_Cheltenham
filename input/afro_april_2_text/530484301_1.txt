COLORED TEACHERS
FOR CHELTENHAM
Rev. Geo. F. Bragg, Member
of Board, Says This
Change Should
Be Made
EFFORT BEGUN IN 1903
Two Colored Teachers Sent
There Did Remarkable
Work
The first and most important
change which should be made at
the House of Reformation for Boys
at Cheltenham is to give the school
colored teachers.
Rev. Dr. George F.Bragg, Jr., a
member of the Board made this
statemem to the Afro-American
today. He also declared that a
State like Maryland which is com-
mitted to the policy of separate
colored schools and colored teach-
ers should see that the policy is
also carried om at Cheltenham.
This step might easily be brought
about he said providing represen-
tative persons make this request of
Mayor Jackson and Governor
Ritchie. Rev. Dr. Braggs state-
ment follows:
20 YEARS ON BOARD
I was elected a member of the
Board of Managers of the House
of Reformation in 1901, and have
been re-elected every year since
that time. From the very begin-
ning I have been under the convic-
tion that the several schools there
should be taught by colored per-
sons.
Through my persistency in the
matter, 20 years ago, a beginning
was made in that direction. Gen.
Shryock strongly favored it. I am
sending herewith an extract from
a personal letter received by me
from Mrs. Ance E. Mitchell a col-
ored body whom I secured as
teacher. Mr. Mitchell, her husband,
was placed in charge of the chair
caning department. The contents
of the letter are most suggestive of
the present, notwithstanding the
letter was written 20 years ago.
The date of the letter is Sept. 5,
1903.
NEW SUPERINTENDENT
In part it says: "Today's Afro-American
states your return: I hope your vacation
has been both pleasurable and profitable.
Of course, you are aware of the changes.
Mr. Pyles is now acting Superintendent.
The boys are even now under better control,
Gen. Shryock, having been down and one
evening called all the boys together and
grandly and firmly, announced his intention
of running the affairs of Cheltenham, laid
down the rules to all officers and boys, and
already good results are attained. There
are now two schools, with the larger boys,
and two with the small boys. Gen.
Shryock recommended sort of segregation. I
have now the smallest boys, to in number,
with the exception of a few retained
helpers, so as not to cripple the work.
"There had been a noticeable degree of
improvement in my school, as a whole.
Or within the past two months. Am pleased
to undertake the helping of the smallest
boys before they become hardened. I find
many of them impressionable, and fit
months for their intellectual moral
and physical advancement.
There is a whole chapter of happenings,
I will not weary you with detail, but be-
ing fully aware of your loyalty and struck
for the right preaching of our race being
an over-prejudiced people, I wait merely
relate to you the parting of Mr.
Dorsey had resigned
with us.
Superintendent.
"Extending his hand, and bidding
both "good-bye", Mr. Dorsey said: "I wish
you in all your future efforts. I
must say you have done much for source:
you, surely, have raised my estimation of
your race, and aroused in me a hope for
them that I never had before. I am glad
to have known you, and to know that in
your care are such people." My reply was
"Thank you, Mr. Dorsey, but you probably
have been most unfortunate in your con-
tact with your race. Among us are many
superior to me, farther advanced, in every
way, I came here merely do my duty al-
ways.
CONFESSION OF DISTRUST
"Thus ran a conversation that was alike
pleasing and sure surprising, with its con-
fession of distrust of our race, and appar-
ent conversion to behalf and hope.
I said to John (her husband) our coming
has been worth while: that we have lived
to see and hear these words, uninvited,
from Mr. Dorsey's lips. God surely is
with us, and here is one reward for faith-
fulness to His laws. Mr. and Mrs. Dor-
sey made us a friendly call before leaving."
MAYOR HAYES PLEASED
I think the above letter is
worthy of re-production at this
time. In company with Mayor
Hayes, I as well as others, visited
Mrs. Mitchell's school, and the
Mayor was specially pleased and
frankly and heartily expressed
himself in favor of colored teach-
ers for these boys. From the very
first I advocated such a change
and all during my membership on
the board have never lost an op-
portunity to urge the same. That
is the one thing in connection with
this institution that should be done.
As for Mr. Pyles I have nothing
but words of praise. I think, un-
der all the circumstances, he has
bravely striven to do his duty. But
it is a situation, and not a theory,
which confronts us, and I can con-
ceive of no good reason for the re-
tention of white teachers in the
schools of that institution.
Mr. and Mrs. Mitchell did splen-
did work, but after some years of
service, the isolation was too great
for them. Such would not be the
case if ALL the teachers were of
the same race as the boys of that
institution. The boys would be
brought into the life of the best of
their own race, and, reflecting the
same, would be transformed into
the same likeness.
