Morgan College may collect a
debt that has been owed to it since
1890 by the University of Mary-
land and is said to now be approxi-
mately $10 $100,000, when the next
session of the legislature convenes
in January, 1935, according to in-
formation received from Harry W.
Nice, governor-elect, this week.
During an interview, Thursday,
Mr. Nice stated that he is in favor
of paying the money to Morgan
at the rate of $25,000 a year.
The bill was incurred when the
University of Maryland sought to
get Federal funds made available
by the Morrell Act. The officials
learned, however, that they could
get no money unless they provided
educational opportunities for col-
ored citizens as well as white.
Three alternatives were offered
to the University of Maryland.
These were: admittance of col-
ored students to the university;
founding of a state institution for
colored students, or making an
agreement with a college within
the state boundaries under which
colored persons would be edu-
cated at state expense.
The University of Maryland
chose the last of these three, and
the Princess Anne Academy at
Princess Anne, Md., was turned
over for the teaching of agricul-
tural and mechanical courses.
No Sale Made
No actual sale was ever made,
but the agreement was considered
a gentleman's agreement.
In 1919 another agreement was
made under which the Univer-
sity of Maryland is required to ask
the state legislature to buy Prin-
cess Anne Academy, whenever
Morgan College requests that this
be done.
In the last three sessions of the
legislature Morgan has been trying
to get the University of Maryland
to live up the the terms of its
agreement, but has not been suc-
cessful on any occasion.
Other Changes Expected
.. Other changes are expected to
take place in civic circles by
the time the legislature is in ses-
sion. Among these changes will
be the taking over of Cheltenham
by the state.
The board of this institution,
which is devoted to the reform of
delinquent colored boys, has
strongly objected to state control.
It is reported that continued op-
position to state control of the in-
stitution by the board will cause
the legislature to withdraw a con-
tribution given each year by the
state.
Further reports indicate that
two colored physicians will be
given posts in state institutions
under the Republican guberna-
torial administration.
A shake-up in the personnel of
several institutions that serve col-
ored citizens is also predicted.
