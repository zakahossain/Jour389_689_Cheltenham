MELVALE IS
DEPLORABLE
Secretary of City Super-
visors Says Funds are
Insufficient.
WASHES HIS HANDS
Janney Says State Has
No Responsibility.
Secretary Nathaniel Grasty,
white, of the board of City
Supervisors, admitted that de-
plorable conditions exist at
Melvale, while Stuart S:
Janney, white, chairman of
the State Department of Wel-
fare, washed his hands in a
declaration that the state had
no control over the place, in
interviews with AFRO re-
porters this week.

Recent criticisms of the place by
Judge J. S. Waxter and Mrs. Marie
Bauernschmidt had not reached Mr.
Janney until called to his attention
by articles in the AFRO-AMERICAN,
he said.
Is Deplorable
"I am conversant," said Mr. Grasty,
"with the conditions at Melvale re.
garding which Judge Waxter and
Mrs. Bauernschmidt have spoken.
Conditions at the institution are de-
plorable."
"Baltimore city has no other al-
ternative but to send colored giris to
the House of the Good Shepherd,
which is overtaxed, to Melvale or to
turn them loose on the streets.
"In answer to your question as to
what can be done about the condi-
tions existing there, I will say that
the appropriations are regulated by
the Board of Estimates. The city
charities cannot give any more to the
institution than is appropriated by
the Board of Estimates.
$200 Per Capita
"The supervisors contract with all
reformatory institutions at the rate
of $200 per capita per child, with the
exception of the Maryland Training
School for Girls and the Maryland
Training School for Boys. These two
schools receive $180 per child. Mel-
vale, like other institutions, receives
$200- per child.
"The charity supervisors are in
sympathy with the welfare of the
girls at Melvale. We believe that the
management is doing the best it can
with the money at its disposal. I
feel that if the board of management
at Melvale is unable to get along on
the money it has, some plan should
be worked out by which the appropri-
ations would be more liberal.
"The public does not have enough
interest in an instituption like Mel-
vale to go into their pockets. Those
girls out there have souls, and should
have a future before them."
Washes Hands
In his statement, Mr. Janney prac-
tically washed his hands of the con-
ditions there. Said he:
"The state has nothing at all to
do with the management or the
handling of Melvale. But if condi-
tions at the institution are as Judge
Waxter and Mrs. Bauernschmidt say,
I certainly wish it was under the
state. We would clean up the place.
"Judge Waxter is a mighty fine
man, and if he says Melvale is de-
plorable, it must be the truth.
"This is the first time that the mat-
ter has been brought to my atten-
tion. I would like to see something
done about, but the state has no ju-
risdiction over the institution."
Dr. Emil Novak
Dr. Einil Novak, a member of the
city board, declared that nothing had
come to his attention about deplor-
able conditions at Melvale. The mat-
ter will come up at a board meeting
this month, he said, at which time it
will be given proper consideration.
Grand Jury Action.
Dr. Wm. H. Wright, present mem-
ber of the grand jury. declared this
week that conditions at both Melvale
and Cheltenham will be given serious
attention by that body. A detailed
study is being made by him and.
recommendations for some definite
action will assuredly he made. he said.
The Urban League committee ap-
pointed last week is studying the
whole situation and will work out a
plan for a practical solution of the
problem. it is said. That body is also
planning to make as its chief work
this year. consideration of welfare
institutions and their administra-
tion in city and state.
