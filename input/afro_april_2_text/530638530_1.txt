Cheltenham Worse Than Pen,
Relatives Can't See Boys
That Cheltenham is worse than
a penitentiary in that relatives are
not permitted to see boys sent there,
is the complaint of Mrs. Edith James
804 South 48th street, Phildelphia.
in a letter to the AFRO-AMERICAN
this week.
Mrs. James' brother, Paul A.
Cook, sent to Cheltenham at his mo-
ther's death, doesn't know that his
father is dead because authorties
there have refused to let his sister
see him or write to him. The where
abouts of young Cook were discov-
ered by relatives through the AFRO.
AMERICAN.
Mrs. James found out that he was
put out to work in October 1917
and will become of age in October
of this year.
In a letter to the AFRO-AMERI-
CAN this week she says that the
action of the authorities in keep-
ing her from seeing or communi-
cating with her brother is an out-
rage and asks under what state law
the authorities are proceeding.
In her letter is enclosed also a
communication from W. H. Janney,
secretary of the House of Reform-
ation at Cheltenham, poorly typed
on the official stationery and signed
also on the typewriter in which
Janney states that young Cook has
been hired out on his own request.
"We have found that it is bad
for boys so placed out to come in
contact with their people because
these poeple visit them, give them
money which they do not need,
correspond with them and in gen-
eral get the boys, dissatisfied with
their surroundings no matter how
good they may be, with the result,
in every case, that the boys run a-
way causing much trouble and ex-
pense to recaptare them, and worse
than-all, forfeit all the money that
has been accumulated in our hands
for their benefit.
"In the case of Paul Cook we
have had considerable trouble along
this line. Not that he has run a-
way, but I think he was preparing
to do so, and I had to send our
agent to see him and get him
straightened out. This was, I think.
caused by some visits to him by a
supposed brother, of which visits
we knew nothing, but it is evident
that there has been some influence
brought to bear on the boy to make
him believe that he could do much
better if he were away from his
present home.
"Therefore we have declined to
tell where the boy is located, be-
cause it is always better for them
not to be, visited by their people
while they are at service, as you
will see from the above, and when
they become of age, all the infor-
mation that we have as to their rel-
atives is given them, their money is
turned over to them and they are
sent to their people."
