Note from Inmate
Cites Brutality
af Cheltenham
Boy Charges That Clothing
Is Taken, and Food Is
Unfit for Dogs.
(Reprinted from late editions of last week)
The charges that clothing is tak-
en, brutal beatings administered,
and poor food is served were
lodged this week against Chelten-
ham, the reform school for boys,
by an inmate whose name is being
withheld by the AFRO.
The contents of the letter telling
of conditions are:
"I am an inmate at the Chelten-
ham reformatory.
"People of Maryland don't know
what the boys go through down
here. Last week a boy died after
being beaten and another was
beaten over the head with a club.
"The food that is cooked for us
is not fit for dogs, but when high
officials come down we can't tell
them the real way we get along
because we are beaten when they
leave if we do.
Boys' Suits Taken.
"If a boy comes here with a
suit that is worth $25, it is taken
away from him and the first boy
to go home gets it. The same
thing is done with shoes.
"When the boys are drilling, if
one of them drops out he is left
under a tree until he comes to.
"The officers had a still down
here, and some of the boys who
found it were beaten with sticks
and locked up on bread and water.
"A boy was writing a story to
your paper and another boy told
on him. He was badly beaten. If
I get out of here, I'll be good, all-
right.
Some Fall in Water.
"In the winter we cut ice on the
water and some fall in, they get
beaten but when people come
down here they don't see any-
thing.
"I am not telling who I am or
how I got this letter out of here,
but I am begging the people of
Baltimore and the State of Mary-
land to see about the boys in
Cheltenham.
"We were bad boys at home but
thouse of reformation is just
as bad."
Judge Waxter Speaks.
Judge Thomas J. S. Waxter, of
the juvenile court, was allowed to
read the letter. Some parts of it
were unintelligible because of the
way in which it was written, but
he asserted that conditions were
bad at Cheltenham.
He declared that a preliminary
report on a survey that is being
made had disclosed many interest-
ing things, and he also stated that
the next session of the legislature
should make the place a state
institution.
Judge Waxter believes that the
institution should be manned
largely by a colored personnel,
and he stated that the report so
far is "loaded with dynamite."
This report is being assembled
by a representative of the Child
Welfare League of America.
Grand Jury Urged Change.
In the report of January 4, the
grand jury recommended that col-
ored officers be placed at the re-
formatory, and that the state take
over the control of it.
It was also pointed out that the
officials had a difficult job to per-
form in the taking care of boys
who are physically unsound,
feeble-minded, tubercular, and
delinquent.
The jurymen declared that the
food, at a noon meal which was
witnessed, was limited in variety
although of a wholesome quality.
