By RALPH MATTHEWS
NEW YORK CITY--There
will be no hi-de-ho in the soul
of dapper Leroy Maxey,
drummer in Cab Calloway's
band, when he returns to
Harlem from Hollywood, if
Mrs. Sadie Maxey, a beauti-
cian and former entertainer,
has her way.
Mrs. Maxey, who says that
she is indirectly responsible
for the creation of the Callo-
way orchestra, has filed
claims for back alimony,
which she says will run into
big money.
Her husband was ordered some-
time ago to pay her $15 per week
from his salary of $100 per week.
Ever since the organization
left the East more than three
months ago, and long prior to
this date, she says she has been
neglected.
She did not bother, she said,
when her husband sent her a re-
ceipt for nearly $600 which he had
spent on his teeth.
Posed as Wife
The straw that broke the cam-
el's back, however, was a story she
read in a Western paper, an-
nouncing the arrival of Mr. and
Mrs. Maxey on the Coast, and she
investigated and learned that an-

Given Summons
To make matters worse, she
states, a process-server called
at her home and attempted to
serve her. with a summons in
connection with unpaid furni-
ture bills and household arti-
cles purchased in the name of
Mrs. Theresa Maxey, who was
her rival and not herself.
She had to produce witnesses to
prove her own identity to avoid
appearance in court.
Helped Make Band
Mrs. Maxey, who was herself
an entertainer and the daughter
of Mr. and Mrs. George Treadwell,
one of the old-line families of
Flushing, Long Island, met and
was married to her husband in
1923.
She met Maxey while playing
with Lew Leslie's "Dixie to Broad-
way" company, first starring ve-
hicle of Florence Mills. Maxey
was the member of an orchestra
known as Robinson's Syncopators
in St. Louis, Mo.
Upon returning to New
York, the then Miss Tread-
well used her influence to
have the orchestra placed in
the Cotton Club at a salary in-
crease of from $40 per week
to $100 per week per man. The
couple were then married.
Went on Strike
After some time in the club, the
band went on strike for $150 per
week, and it was then that Irvin
Mills snatched the obscure Duke
Ellington and his five-piece band
from a smaller club, increased the
organization and placed them in
the Cotton Club.
The St. Louis boys were then
without their name of Cotton Club
Orchestra, and assumed the name
of the Missourians, the name
which they carried until Cab Cal-
loway was placed in control and
finally the name, "Cab Calloway
and His Missourians" became just
Cab Calloway's Orchestra.
In "Africana"
Before this transition took place,
however, Mrs. Maxey again re-
sorted to all of her New York con-
tacts to place the band again and
had them spotted with "Africana,"
produced by Earl Dancer, with
Ethel Waters as the star. This
was in 1927.
All of these things Mrs.
Maxey did, she said, out of
love for her husband, and ev-
erything was rosy until her
husband outgrew her and
wrote on the back of their
marriage license. "I do not
want my wife, Sadie Tread-
well Maxey, any more."
$3,000 Drums
Even after this, she still played
the part of a friend, she said, and
it was her father who went on the
note when her husband purchased
his $3,000 set of trap drums, which
is said to be one of the finest
rhythm sets in Harlem music cir-
cles.
When Mrs. Maxey learned that
her husband had a traveling com-
panion, she immediately burned
the wires to California, demanding
an explanation.
Denies Report
She received a wire saying:
"Don't believe what you
read in jig papers. They don't
get anything right."
Nevertheless, Mrs. Maxey and
all Harlem are waiting the return
of the prince of hi-de-ho and his
crew, who are scheduled for a date
at the 125th Street Apollo Theatre
within a few weeks' time.
