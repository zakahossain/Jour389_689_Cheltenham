Hurley, the accused guard, ac-

Pine-Box Threat
"I don't like your looks." Bron-
son said the guard gave as his

"You are not tough, just rough,

"Because he seemed to be
drunk, I didn't take him serious-
ly," Bronson said, "and I went on
to my table and ate my meal.
After supper, Guard Hurley came
back to the boys' dining room to
see whether I had eaten at table
number five, and when he learned
that I didn't, informed me that
he would see me later."
Convinced
Bronson said that when he
went to the dormitory, he was
convinced that Hurley was drunk,
and, being afraid that he might
hurt him, he asked Hurley to give
him a permit to see the superin-
tendent, John Pyles. This re-
quest was refused after asking
Hurley twice, Bronson charged.
Fired Over Guard
"I then turned to go to the
school room," Bronson said, and
as I did so, Hurley raised a stick
to hit me, and I caught it. At
that time, Guard Greer stepped
in between us and took the stick
away from Hurley. Hurley then
said, 'Let me at him,' and, reach-
ing over Greer's shoulder. fired
one shot into my chest," Bronson
told the AFRO.
After he was shot, Bronson
said, he ran down stairs, refus-
ing to come back at the request
of the two guards. Later, he said,
one of his fellow inmates carried
him to the reformatory hospital.

Bronson is serving his third
sentence in the House of Refor
mation, he told the AFRO. He
was sent there in 1924 for stay-
ing out late at night, for which
he served three years. In 1923,
he served a year for playing
hookey, and in 1929, he was re-
turned to the reformatory on a
charge of disorderly conduct. Last
February he ran away from the
home, he said, and was captured
and taken back last June.
Bronson said he is 21 years of
age, and is due to be released
from the reformatory, but the of-
