MARYLAND INDUSTRIAL
SCHOOL FOR BOYS
Such is the name and title of an
institution which has been proposed
by His Honor Judge Williams of the
Juvenile Court, of the city of
Baltimore. The bill which has
already been prepared will be in-
troduced in the State Legislature.
The measure has the heartiest
support of the Ministerial Alliance
of this city. Judge Williams ap
peared before this body. upon its
invitation, at the December meet-
ing, and outlined the features of
the measure, and the Alliance not
only warmly indorsed the project,
but appointed a committee, repre-
senting the various denominations
of colored citizens, to communicate
with the Governor about the mat-
ter, and to cooperate with the
Judge in such ways as he should
deem most helpful.
The bill provides that the in-
stitution shall be located in the
country, not more than 15 miles
remote from this city; it appropri-
ates twenty-five thousand dollars
for the acquiring of the plant, and
ten thousand dollars annually, from
the State of Maryland, and five
thousand from the city of Balti-
a
more, towards its maintainence
and support. Colored boys between
the ages of 8 and 18 may be com-
mitteed to the institution by the
Judge of the Juvenile Court, or by
other judicial authority through-
out the State. It needs no argu-
ment whatever to establish the
necessity of such an institution.
We are devoutly glad that it comes
from such
man as Judge
Williams. Apart from his con-
nection with the Juvenile Court,
and the splendid opportunities thus
afforded to judge of the real
necessity of such an institution,
Judge Williams is a godly man,
and a true type of the Christian
gentleman, and so impressed were
the colored preachers with his
sympathetic personality, that were
it in their power they would make
him Judge of the Juvenile Court
for life. And, while writing on
this subject, it comes to our mind,
from personal knowledge, that
there is a large army of such
benevolently disposed white citi-
zens who share in the same sincere
and devoted feelings towards their
colored brethren, as our friend
Judge Williams. But, the great
trouble is, they do not touch the
lives of colored citizens at any
point, and colored people, generally
speaking, are wholly ignorant of
their existence. Now is the oppor-
tunity for some of the white states-
men, at Annapolis, who have, in so
many ways, wrought an impression
upon colored people, that they are
entirely indifferent to anything
which pertains to them, save re-
strictive legislation, to vindicate
the good and redeeming side of
their nature. And so we devoutly
hope that they may embrace the
present opportunity as an earnest
of their good intentions. In con-
nection with this same subject, it
is most timely that we should note
the good work of a white country-
man, who has proven a real bene-
factor of the colored race in Mary-
land. And, this we shall do in the
article headed "Cheltenham.".
