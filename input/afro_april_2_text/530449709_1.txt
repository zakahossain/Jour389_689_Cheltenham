CHELTENHAM NOT
AN IDEAL SPOT
E. B. Taylor, Members of
Board of Managers, An-
swers Editorial in Last
Week's Afro
POOR CONDITIONS ADMITTED
Boys Have To Work Out
Because State Does Not
Provide Sufficient Funds
The House of Correction at Chel-
tenham is not an ideal institution
any more than the Colored High
School, declared E. B. Taylor, mem-
ber of the Board of Managers in
reply to an editorial in last week's
AFRO-AMERICAN.
The editorial commented on the
lack of any useful educational sys-
tem at Cheltenham, the convict plan
of leasing boys to work on adjoining
farms, and ended by declaring:
Two colored members are on the
Board of Managers of Cheltenham.
No part of the criticism appears to
have touched them sufficiently to
make a public statement. Is their
silence an evidence of their satis-
faction with conditions at Chelton-
ham?
HAS NOT KEPT SILENT
Denying that he had kept silent
or was satisfied with Cheltenham,
Mr. Taylor pointed out that he had
recently printed an article in the
AFRO-AMERICAN concerning condi-
tions there and therefore could not
be accused of keeping silent.
"I admit," Mr. Taylor said "that
only two trades are taught at the
House of Correction, that the boys
are hired out to work on adjacent
farms, and that the schooling does
not proper them for good citizen-
ship when they leave, but the fault
lies with the colored people them-
selves. They should be behind the
members on the Board, and should
appeal to the Governor and other
State authorities to give Chelten-
ham an appropriation large enough
to operate them properly.
"If we did not hire boys out to
work, there would not be sufficient
money with which to run the insti-
tution.
BOARD INVESTIGATED
"A letter in the AFRO-AMERICAN by
James Ireland declaring that the
boys were mistreated by Superin-
tendent John B. Pyles, that he lay
in the shade neglecting his duties
and compelled boys to fan him, and
that one of the boys had killed an-
other, was investigated by our Board
which spent a day on the grounds.
The AFRO-AMERICAN was invited to
this investigation."
When informed that the AFRO-
AMERICAN knew nothing of an in-
vestigation and was hearing of it for
the first time, Mr. Taylor said he
understood from President Janney
of the Board that the AFRO-AMERICAN
had been asked to be present if it
wished.
Mr. Taylor said that the investiga-
tion proved that the murder was
committed and the boy was public-
ly tried for the offense. No one could
be found, however, who would sub-
stantiate charges against the super-
intendent.
"Anyone," he said, "can claim that
there is mistreatment of boys and
shad food at Cheltenham, but unless
he is willing to come before the
Board and press his charges, his
claims are worthless.
UP TO COURTS
Asked to comment upon the pres-
ent conditions by which white chil-
dren may be committed to institu-
tions for 20 or 60 days while all
colored children are committed to
Cheltenham until they become 21
years of age, Mr. Taylor said this
matter is beyond the province of
the Board. "It is up to the Courts,"
he said. "If you want these things
changed, it is up to the people to
ask judges not to send boys to Chel-
tenham until conditions are like you
would have them."
