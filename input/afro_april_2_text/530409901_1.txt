CHELTENHAM
Cheltenham is situated in Prince
George's county, Md. Located at
that place, is an institution for
colored boys, known as the House
of Reformation. The name of
Enoch Pratt is not only connected
with the public libraries of our
city, but also with this institution
for colored boys at Cheltenham.
The superintendent of the House
of Reformation is Mr. John B.
Pyles, a white man, and a native
of that county. He is a man of
a kind heart, but brave and
energetic. His is a great task, but
he is perfectly at home in the doing
of the work devolving upon him.
He is, really, a benefactor of the
Negro race. just where such bene-
faction is most needed. But, we
started this article simply to call
attention to the fact, that Chelten-
ham, in operation, is more of a
great industrial school, than a
"Reformatory." Some of the
many colored boys who enter the
portals of Cheltenham receive for
the first time a knowledge of the
primary lessons in civilization.
Good food, warm clothing, school
and work; and all under proper
discipline.
Very recently, a colored tailor,
Mr. J. P. Featherstone, of this
city, succeeded the former tailor
of the institution who was white.
We are pleased to learn that Mr.
Featherstone is not only giving
satisfaction, but is making a good
reputation for himself. In a pri-
vate letter to one of the members
of the Board of Managers, Mr.
Featherstone writes, in part, as
follows:
"The head of my service seems
rather much pleased up to the
present. I have already turned
out 70 blue pants, 50 blue coats,
65 grey pants, 18 grey coats, 36
top shirts, 45 under drawers, with
required repairs for entire school.
There are 12 boys in my depart-
ment;6 machines and 6 bench boys.
Two of good behavior, four in
moods of behavior, and the other
six very troublesome; but 1 am
able to keep them, so far, in
thorough subjection, and every
authority is given me in the com-
plete circle of my charges."
Mr. Featherstone seems to be
the right man in the right place.
We are glad of this, because when
other vacancies exists, if colored
men, of the proper moral and
efficient type can be found, they
will have vouchsafed them the
same opportunity Mr. Featherstone
is so well and wisely using. Two
things more. From the above
sample, the reading colored com-
munity can get some idea of the
value and importance of the work
at Cheltenham. It is a little sur-
prising that so few, almost none,
representative colored people ever
visit this institution. Such not
only have a standing invitation,
but they are sure to become en-
thusiastic supporters of the insti-
tution, if once they visit the place.
Finally, the only way to learn how
to manage such a great institution
is by the race availing itself of
such opportunities as, now and
then, become open as indicated
in the filling of the place of tailor.
Let the race have the right kind of
men for such openings whenever
they shall occur.
