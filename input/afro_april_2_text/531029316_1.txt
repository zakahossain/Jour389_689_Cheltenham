BALTIMORE, Md.-
That the increasing crime rate
could not be ascribed to any one
cause, but to several mutually inter-
dependent causes, Mrs. Armamita
W. Anthony, of the department of
crime prevention of New York City,
told members of the Co-Operative
Women's Civic League, Saturday.
Mrs. Anthony spoke from theli
subject, "The Work of the Crime
Prevention Bureau Among Negroes
in Harlem," at the social service
conference, held by the league at
the Booker T. Washington Junior
High School.
Poverty Blamed
Present day crime is characterized
by poverty, low economic status, low
wages, lack of work and overcrowd-
ing of homes, which form a back
ground for the large percentage of
criminals she said.
"Even in prosperous times colored
mothers, more often than others,
are employed outside of the home.
All of us are familiar with the
youngster wearing his door key
about his neck. What then is to be
expected but that children deprived
of this parental supervision should
get much of their training from the
streets?
"Although this lack of parental
supervision due to poverty is preca-
rious enough, it cannot be mcre se-
rious than the poverty brought on
by unemployment.
"With welfare agencies taxed to
their utmost to furnish the bare
necessities, there is nothing left for
recreations, or other things that
children consider necessary to their
afe.
Need Recreation
"Movies, for instance, have be-
come almost as necessary to the av-
erage child as food, clothes ad
shelter. Children will steal in or-
der to get what adults may consider
pure luxuries.
"Large families forced into over-
crowded rooms and tenements are
obviously a menace. Indiscriminate
herding of both sexes lays the foun-
dation for future immorality.
"Then many children are being.
forced to leave school and are thus
inadequately prepared to find work.
Even when they are prepared, they
cannot, at all times, find work.
Hence they seek excitement which
often proves hazardous. A stolen
car, a ride with a stranger, or a
questionable dance may be the be-
ginning of a criminal career.
"Children in poor or crowded sec-
tions often fail to get that sympa-
thetic understanding necessary to
formative years.
"The work in crime prevention in
Harlem has shown that children's
behavior isn't always due to pure
cussedness, but is a result of some
profound maladjustment, physical
or mental handicap or bad inheri-
tance. Many times these can be
prevented if observed at an early
age, thus preventing criminal atti-
tudes.
"Thus, no single cause can be
pointed to as being the factor in
any criminal situation. With ten-
tacles reaching far into the com-
munity, correlated factors wind and
rewind themselves into resulting
misconduct."
In explaining the work of her de-
partment, Mrs. Anthony pointed out
that the moment cases came under
the observance of that department,
they ceased to be ploice cases but
became individual problems of mal-
adjustment. To prevent embar-
rassment, no uniformed officer is
sent on the case. In many instan-
ces, Mrs. Anthony said, children
were most co-operative, and in some
instances brought friends to the de-
partment whom they thought in
danger.
Mrs. Sarah Collins Fernandis.
president of the league, presided at
the social conference session.
The discussion following the ad-
dress by Mrs. Anthony was opened
by Edward S. Lewis, executive sec
retary of the Urban League, who
pointed out the lack of colored so-
cial service workers under the muni-
cipality of the city of Baltimore.
Mr. Lewis urged the league to lend
its support to the effort to secure
workers, and colored policeman and
policewomen.
Boys Under Peonage
In a report on the Cheltenham
School for Boys, Mr. Lewis decried
the lack of up-to-date trade train-
ing equipment. The broom factory,
he said, was tuberculosis breeding
because of its lack of air cleaning
facilities, and that many of the in-
mates were in a state of literal
peonage to white farmers.
"We must have laboratory studies
made by members of our own group
for we have problems which no
white person can ever reach," Mr.
Lewis said.
Mr. Lewis also pointed out that
the type of supervision at Chelten-
ham does not make for betterment
of the inmates. The people in
charge are not of the fine scientific
type, he said, but the kind who de-
pend upon the "big stick" for-dis-
cipline.
A talk on the work of the Child
Study Group in the city was given
by Clarence Roberts, principal of
School No. 128. Mr. Roberts re-
ported that Schools Nos. 110, 118,
102, 115, 125, 129, 101 and 113 had
branches of the Child Study Group.
"Realizing that our biggest need
is for improved citizenship," Mr.
Roberts said, "and for appreciation
of the finer things in life, the group
has set itself to work to counteract
slip-shod training. The work of the
group consists of studying the ac-
tions of children to note whether
these actions would lead to fuller
lite or to juvenile court and de-
linquency and to study the methods
of coping with such actions."
