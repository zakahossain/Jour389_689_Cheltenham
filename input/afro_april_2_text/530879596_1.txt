Grand Jury Says White

MELVALE LACKING
Better Care for Convicts
than Children
The State of Maryland is
more concerned about the wel-
fare of hardened criminals
confined in the penitentiary
and the House of Correction
than it is about the guidance
and comfort of the unfortu-
nate boys and girls placed in
its care at Cheltenham and
Melvale, a report of the grand
jury reveals.
The report on the several institu-
tions of the state contains the fol-
lowing contrasting findings of that
body.
Cheltenham
1. "At Cheltenham we found one
dormitory, housing 50 boys with beds
of iron. sadly in need of cleaning and
paintng. The building had but one
toilet and one washroom.
2. "There were but two kinds of
endeavor, farming and the broom
factory, which gave the boys no op-
portunity for initiative, which leaves
them sulky and discontented. The
broom factory was crowded without
enough space to breathe; the flying
chaff in the stuffy quarters hampers
the respiration and breeds tubercu-
losis.
3. "The food was served by bare-
foot boys in a crude manner. (Note-
This visit was made in February, a
winter month.)
4. "There were no inspiring mot-
toes, books or any inducements to
aspire to better things and the school-
ing was a farce."
The Penitentiary
1. "At the Penitentiary we found
the living quarters clean, comfortable
and an order prevailing that showed
the inmates were as contented as it
were possible to be under such cir-
cumstances.
2. "The inmates seemed interested
in their work and many were appre-

The Baltimore City Jail
"At the Baltimore City Jail food
was plentiful and served in a clean
sanitary manner. The building was
clean and the inmates were apparent-
ly contented."
Urge Colored Personnel
The jury recommended that the
state purchase the institution
at
Cheltenham which is now privately
owned and man it with a colored
personnel. The conditions now exist-
ing, they point out, are deplorable,
due to the fact that the white em-
ployees are wholly lacking in the un-
derstanding and sympathy so neces-
sary to the rehabilitation of youth.
If colored are employed, racial ties
and sympathy would do much to cor-
rect the evils now existing and would
be a great stride forward for human-
ity.
At Melvale
The committee designated to in-
spect Cheltenham was composed of
Eli L. M. Fishpaw, chairman, Israel
Silverstein, Joseph O. Barger, John
Brendel and Dr. William H. H. Wr Wright.
The same group visited the Mel-
vale school for girls and declared that
they found this the poorest of the
eleven state institutions visited, and
said that the state is sadly falling
short of its obligations to colored girls.
