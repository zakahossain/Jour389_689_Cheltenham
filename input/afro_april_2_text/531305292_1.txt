Cheltenham
Fight Lost
BALTIMORE
Voting as a bloc, the six
white members of the board
of management of Chelten-
ham School for Boys elect-
Led Claude D. Jones, 57-
year-old white applicant,
educational director for
that institution on Thurs-
day.
The five colored members pres-
ent voted for Martin J. Brennan,
37, for seventeen years connected
with Hampton Institute, for the
post.
The action of the board, still
dominated by the same group
under which Cheltenham won the
reputation of being one of the!
most shamefully mismanaged re-
form institutions in the country,
dissipates hope, leaders declared
Thursday night, that the O'Conor
administration will develop a
capable colored staff at the insti-
tution.
White members who voted for
Mr. Jones were Enoch Harlan,
Joseph H. Blandford, Hal B. Cla-
gett, Blossie K. Miller, James E.
i Steuart and J. Amos Davis.
i
Colored members who voted for
Mr. Brennan were John L. Berry,
the Rev. Albert J. Mitchell, Miss
Ida R. Cummings and Mrs. Ber
tha B. Proctor.
Not present at the meeting
were Dr. Harry C. Byrd, Howard
C. Hill, Dr. William H. Gibbons,
M. Hampton Magruder, white,
and Dr. J. L. Johnson.
Effort Blocked
The election of Mr. Jones fol-
lowed an exhaustive personnel
report by Col. Harry C. Jones,
State employment commissioner,
in which he gave the three appli-
cants ratings. These ratings, it
was said, placed the applicants in
the following order: Jones, Bren-
non and Charles Kempton.
Superintendent Sunderland, it
was believed, favored the selec-
tion of Mr. Brennon.
The action of the controlling
bloc, therefore, led some of the
colored members to believe that
the selection of the white educa-
tional director meant that at some
future date he would be elevated
to the post of superintendent, now
held by Sunderland.
Second Battle Pending
The white bloc also pegged an
effort on the part of the colored
members to insure that the
physical and recreational direc-
tor, yet to be named, would be
colored.
They voted solidly against a
resolution offered by the Rev.
Mr. Mitchell to the effect that
preference be given a colored
applicant for this post.
Blame O'Conor
Leaders interested in reforms
at Cheltenham who commented
on the selection of the white edu-
cational director on Thursday
blamed Governor Herbert O'Con-
or for the outcome.
They pointed out that his fail-
ure to name new members to the
board when the terms of five
members expired last May left
in control the same group under
which the institution has been so
long mismanaged.
Whether or not, they say, this
action on the part of the Gover-
nor is deliberate, the result has
been to block the appointment of
colored applicants to posts insur-
ing efficient and sympathetic ad-
ministration of the institution in
the interest of the inmates instead
of job-seeking whites.
Promise Unfulfilled
They also state that this action
does not square with the Gover-
nor's promise to give colored citi-
zens a chance to conduct their
own institutions.
The members whose terms ex-
pired last May were Enoch Har-
lan, Howard C. Hill, Dr. J. L.
Johnson, Blossie K. Miller, James
E. Steuart. Miller, Steuart, Har-
lan and Blandford voted for Mr.
Jones on Thursday. Howard C.
Hill has voted generally with the
reform group.
