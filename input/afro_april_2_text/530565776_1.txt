The FORUM
Is The Democratic Party An En-
emy of The Colored Schools?
Rev. Geo. F. Bragg Asks This
Question And Answers It

Mr. Editor, when you were a small
boy, in 1895, we had an important
election in this city and State. By
the most solid vote of Negroes, and
thousands of reputable Democrats,
a Republican was elected Governor,
and a Republican was elected May-
The Legislature was Republi-
can, and the City Council was Re-
publican. The city School Board
was also Republican.
The colored group asked of the
Republican Legislature the found-
ing of a state institution, similar
to Tuskegee, for Colored People of
Maryland. The bill was introdue-
ed, and had a chance of passing:
but our Republican governor, and
his Board of State Education, de-
feated it, on the ground, "there is
no pressing necessity" for the same.
In the City Council, in 1896, the
late Dr. J. Marcus Cargill, intro-
duced a measure to "eliminate"
white teachers from our colored
schools. After a Republican City
Council had amended nearly all the
life out of the bill it finally passed.
In the shape in which it passed, if
honestly and fairly enforced, the
"elimination" would have been ex-
ceedingly slow. But, by actual sub-
terfuge, with a Republican Mayor,
School Board, and Council, every
advantage was taken and the mat-
ter of elimination delayed
Pressure and threats, were brot
to bear upon our white Republicans:
and, tawo years after the bill had
passed the first beginning in the
matter of "elimination" was initi-
ated, during the fall of 1898, when
Henry Grafton Brown, and Miss
Nannie Groomes were put in charge
of the East Street School, in East
Baltimore.
Now, as a contrast with the above.
In the fall of 1899, Thomas Gordon
Hayes, a Democrat, was elected
Mayor over W. T. Malster. Despite
his infidelity in the matter, Malster
received, practically. the united
support of the Colored Poeple. Hay-
es was under no political obligation
to the Colored Race. But soon after
his inauguration the. "New chart-
er" went into effect. A new School
Board, of nine persons, was ap-
pointed by Mr. Hayes, and it was
this School Board appointed by the
Democratic Mayor which wrought
a radical change in the Colored
Schools. It was this board that
gave us Prof. Hugh Brown, and an
entire colored faculty in our High
School.
Mr. Hooper, as Mayor, had the
opportunit to appoint a colored
man on the Cheltenham Board: but.
did not do it. Mr. Malster had the
same opportunity, but appointed
two white men; and when his at-
tention was called to the matter he
promised to do better next time.
But one of his appointees declined,
and he immediately appointed an-
other white man, a Mr. Doyle, a
whiskey dealer.
Now for the contrast, Thomas G.
Hayes, a Democratic Mayor, was
the first of the Mayors of Balti-
more City to make such an appoint-
ment. He appointed Gen. Shry-
rock and Harry S. Cummings to
represent the city of Baltimore on
that Board. Timanus, Republi-1
can Mayor, made no appointments
of a colored man on the board of
that institution.
