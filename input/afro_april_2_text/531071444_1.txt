Disclosure Follows Recent
Shooting of Boy.
CHELTENHAM, Md. Reports
that youthful inmates of the
Cheltenham Reformatory
were
beaten frequently and unmerci-
fully by white guards at the in-
stitution were denied Tuesday by
Superintendent John Pyles, white,
following an investigation by
representatives of the AFRO.
Only last week, news was pub-
lished of the serious shooting of
Aubrey Brunson, Baltimorei
youth, by one of the white guards
at the institution, precipitated,
according to reports, by an argu-
ment over the boy's disobedience
of a minor order.
The investigation was prompted
by written accounts of an alleged
beating of Louis Waters of Balti-
more, said to have been admin-
istered recently by three white
guards, in a basement room at
the institution after the youth was
reported to have disobeyed the
orders of one of the guards.
Eye-Witnesses Give Account
Eye-witnesses, when interview-
ed by an AFRO reporter, said
that the Waters boy was locked
in a cell for punishment, instead
of being taken to the superintend-
ent's office, after he retaliated
when a guard, under whose di-
rection he was working in a to-
bacco barn, struck the youth.
Inmates, in their description of
the incident, declared that a schada
(whipping implement made with
several plies of leather), steel
knuckles, a taped stick and fists
were used to beat the youth un-
til his head was a "mass of
blood.'
Blinded, Led from Cell
They stated that Waters was
unable to see, when finally re-
leased from the cell, and had to
be led to the office before being
taken to a hospital.
When shown a diagram, drawn
by one of the inmates as his ver-
sion of the affair, more than a
dozen other inmates declared that
it was a perfect description of
the manner in which the boys
are handled.
They also stated that the in-
struments of punishment are used
freely by the guards.
Use of Weapons Denied
Superintendent Pyles declared
that no brass knuckles nor black-
jack weapons were used at the
institution. as he carefully looked
at diagram. He showed reporters
the schad and said it was the only
thing used in punishing the boys,
and it was always used in his
presence in the office.
He also stated that the law for
unruly boys is that they be
brought to his office by guards
for investigation. If he finds that
they deserve punishment, he de-
clared, it is administered there.

When reminded that if those
were orders, guards apparently
were assuming additional respon-
sibility when they struck boys,
he said it was all right for them
to do so when they saw fit. He
failed to explain why Waters was
carried to the basement before
being brought to his office.
The superintendent's version of
the Waters affair was as follows:
Waters disobeyed a guard or
did not pile the tobacco in the
manner the guard wanted.
Angered, the guard slapped
Waters and Waters picked up an
stick to defend himself.
Two guards then took him to
the cottage, where he attempted
to jump out a window. A guard
headed him off from the outside
and two of them pulled him back
through the window.
Waters, according to Superin-
tendent Pyles, scratched one of
the guards, and in return the
guard, who had Waters's head
clutched in his arm, used his fist
to free Waters's hold. Mr. Pyles
said that all the blood came from
Waters's nose.
Boys Still Look Down
A white woman came up the
steps behind reporters and, know-
ing the rule that boys must look
down, the reporters watched her.
As she reached the top step,
where a boy was sitting with his
head down, she inquired: "Are
you asleep?"
Another Boy Escapes
Returning to Baltimore Tues-
day, the reporters were stopped
along the road by a frantic youth
who said he had been beaten up
last winter by a guard. He ex-
hibited a broken foot and indi-
cated where several teeth had
been lost, allegedly as a result of
his beating.
He said he had been bounced
against the concrete floor and
had been seeking a chance to get
away for months.
When shown the diagram of the
Waters incident, which included
the name of one of the guards,
the escaping youth said:
"That man has got them
knuckles in the back of his car."
He added: "They drink whiskey,
too." The youth revealed that
he had been sent to Cheltenham
seven years ago and was sup-
posed to stay until he was 21. He
is only 16 now. He said that he
was sent there because he would
not stay at home, but would stay
now rather than return to the re-
formatory.
