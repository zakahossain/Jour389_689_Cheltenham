By ELLIOTT FREEMAN
BOSTON-Hello, everybody!
Once more your reporter is wish-
ing the readers of this column,
and above all those who have
contributed quips and yarns, a
belated happy New Year,
And also, I am thanking from
the bottom of my heart the read-
ers of this column for their Yule-
tide greetings and best wishes for
the success of this reporter.
I am certain that you who
sent in Yuletide greetings realize
that it is physically impossible for
me to extend a personal greet-
ing. Greetings from all parts of
the country, many from people
who gave no address, but who
are readers of this pillar and,
too, many readers of New Eng-
land and this city who forgot to
sign their names.
To all of you, I am extending
my deepest gratitude.

AFRO NEWSIES'
NEW YEAR'S PARTY
In this issue you'll find a pic-
ture of over a score of AFRO
newsies celebrating at their 1941
New Year's party at Little Dixie
Night Club. Friday afternoon.
Over half of the newsies were so
busy serving their customers
with the AFROS that they ar-
rived too late to appear in the
picture, but in time to partake
of the AFRO's dinner celebration,
And everyone of them will tell
you that their party was the best
they ever attended.

THANKS TO LITTLE DIXIE
The owner of Little Dixie, big-
hearted Danny Galiano, not only
provided favors for the kids, but
had the entire show and the
band out to entertain the newsies.
It was their day. And your re-
porter is thanking the waiters,
cooks, doorman, entertainers and
members of the orchestra for
their successful efforts in mak-
ing the 1941 New Year's party
of the Greater Boston AFRO
Newsies the best the old burg
has ever seen.

AMONG THE
BRIGHT LIGHTS
Little Dixie and the Savoy
management are making elabo-
rate preparations to make this
the best season they ever had.
But, with such stars as Little
Dixie is presenting in Angie
Plenty, character actress; Frances
Gatewood, singer of sweet love
songs; Irene Wiley, those dancing
clowns, Snooks and Allen, and
the Phantom Duo, consisting of
Edward Farrow and Fred Law-
son, not to mention the inimi-
table midget emcee, Snowball,
there is no need to give a new
show. It is the best in town
already.
And at the Savoy it is with
regret that we learn Julia Gard-
ner, song bird and accordionist,
is departing for a vacation and
will visit her mother in New
York City. Julia is one of the
best performers. And here is
wishing that Steve Connelly, the
owner, will succeed in the diffi-
cult task of providing a succes-
sor to her.
But folks. the genial chap avers
that he can do so. Candia, torch
singer and dancer, is due to be-
gin her engagement on Thursday,
FIRST NITE
CLUB MANAGER
*
One of the younger girls who
is destined to make good, is pe-
tite Alice Rollins, vocalist with
the Sabby Lewis orchestra. Just
why Angie Plenty does not go
in for a booking agent is a mys-
tery; the girl puts over half the
entertainers who enter town to
work, and absolutely turns
thumbs down on any cut. That
new check girl you see at that
Great White Way hot spot is
Priscilla Martin.
Phil Holmes, my former nite
club waiter, will be surprised be-
cause I am running the follow-
ing lines instead of those he ex-
pects. They are that he was the
recipient of a beautiful sparkler
from his pretty spouse for Christ-
mas. And the lad, who is enter-
ing upon his thirteenth year of
marital bliss, gave his better half
a fur coat.
Aside to Mme. G. L.: The per-
sonnel of the orchestra at Little
Dixie are:
Wilbur Pinckney, first sax; Joe
Craddock, sax: Danny Potter,
sax; William Stanley, trumpet;
David Chestnut, drummer, and
High Diggs, pianist.
That sparkler you see on that
tell-tale finger of glamorous
Mary Murphy, at the Savoy, is a
pledge of matrimony.
