Thus spoke Gov. Harry W. Nice,
Monday, in reiteration of his pre-
election promises in regard to al-
leviating unsatisfactory conditions
at the Cheltenham Reformatory,
against which repeated complaints
have been registered by numer-
ous organizations and individuals.
The governor's declaration was
made following the announcement
that a special committee of the
retiring grand jury had recom-
mended that the institution be
taken over by the state.

"I said in my campaign that I
believed the institution should be
taken over by the state," he de-
clared, "and I purpose to take it
over. The boys there are wards of
the state, and I do not believe the
wards of the state should be
farmed out as is done in this case."
Nice said he was not prepared to
discuss the report of the grand
jury committee, but expressed fa-
miliarity with ger.eral conditions
at the reformatory and mentioned
a survey, now in his possession
but not yet made public, made
through the Board of State Aids
and Charities.
Gov. Nice called attention, in
his statement, to the fact that
Cheltenham is "one of the last
institutions of its kind." Although
a number of institutions for chil-
dren were originally private, the
State of Maryland has taken over
practically all of them except
Cheltenham; the Melvale School
for Girls having gone under state
control in 1931.
Want Pyle Replaced
The grand jury report, submit-
ted to the judges of the criminal
court on Friday, complained of
overcrowding, inadequate medical
facilities, lack of recreational de-
velopment and the probable influ-
ence of older vicious boys on the
younger inmates.
It was recommended that Su-
perintendent Pyle, white, be re-
tired and that he be replaced by a
trained executive. Although the
report failed to discuss the treat-
ment of inmates at the reforma-
tory, against which so many com-
plaints have been lodged, much
emphasis was placed on health
conditions.
