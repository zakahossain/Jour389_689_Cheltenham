BALTIMORE-Gasps of amaze-
ment rippled through Musicians'
Hall, Saturday, as George Clark,
28, unfolded a story of alleged in-
human treatment suffered by the
inmates of Cheltenham.
Clark, who spent the years from
1920 to 1930 in the institution, ex-
hibited scars and pointed to bumps
on his head which he claimed to
have received from the white
guards at Cheltenham. He ap-
peared before a special meeting of
the Abolish Cheltenham, Commit-
tee, called by Mrs. Maud Bell.
Aubrey Brunson, recently shot by
one of the guards, was to have
spoken, but did not appear. How-
ever, those persons seeking infor-
mation as to the reputed abuses
which the young boys undergo were
not disappointed:
was incarcerated.
"Cheltenham is a place not fit
for a dog," declared the former
inmate as he launched into his nar-
ration. "A dog can get water when
he wants it, but those boys can't
get any water until the guard is
good and ready.
"People are told that the boys
have school, but they don't. The
men out there tell the people most
anything."
No School, Is Charge
At this point, Clark told of his
blasted hopes for an education,
which he had hoped to receive at
Cheltenham. "All I saw there was
work," he said.
Speaking of punishment, Clark
told of the leather straps, four and
one-half feet long, which he said
are used to beat the boys. "When
they are ready to whip you," he
continued, "there are two men; one!
has this long strap and the other
has a stick or cane.
Forced to Stoop
"They make you kend over with
your hands touching the floor.
Then they hit you. If you don't
bend over, the men will hit you
all over the face, head and any-
where. Just for talking or any lit-
tle things, the guards send you to
No. 5 table," the speaker continued.
"You just look at the other boys
eat. If you say anything to any-
body, you go right back on No. 5
at the next meal.
"If you keep it up," Clark said
further, "they'll beat you up and
throw you in a cell for thirty-five
or forty days, on bread and water."
The speaker told of a beating
he claims to have received in 1926.
"When the guard struck me with
a stick," he said, "I threw up my
hand to defend myself. I was then
knocked down, struck in the head,
kicked in the ribs-onc being brok-
en. I stayed in the hospital for
six months."
Clark denied statements of the
superintendent that the youths are
taken to the office when they are
whipped. He claimed that when-
ever a guard takes a notion to beat
up one of the boys, it is done.
Vermin in Food
A revolting story of the food
served the youths was related.
"Bugs, dirt and vermin can be
found in the food," Clark stated.
"The boys in the hospital get the
same food as the guards, but they
have to be nearly dead before be-
ing admitted," Clark charged.
The former inmate also told of a
tobacco-chewing white cook, who
allowed the saliva to drip from his
mouth into the soup or other food
he happened to be preparing. Ob-
jection to the man's filthiness or
any other things would only bring
about a severe lashing, Clark told
those assembled.
Editor Pays Visit
How officials at Cheltenham in-
timidate the boys to prevent them
giving out information to visitors
was also related by Clark. The
speaker recalled visits of the grand
juries and how the reputed injus-
tices were kept hidden from them
and other visitors. He also told of
a visit made by Carl Murphy, edi-
tor of the AFRO-AMERICAN,
1925.

The speaker told of fear of talk-
ing with Mr. Murphy, but stated
that he promised to write, which
he did, sending six letters. Front-
page stories of the charges were
carried in the AFRO-AMERICAN.
He related how the officials tried
to find the person who gave out the
information. Clark said that one
of his fellow inmates knew that he
had written the letters, and that
he warned him not to tell about it.
The speaker said that he knew
if the officials learned that he gave
out the information they would
just about kill him and that he
made up his mind to kill the boy
should he tell.
