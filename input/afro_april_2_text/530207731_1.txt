BOYS AND GIRLS WHO GO ASTRAY
What about our boys and girls
who go astray? Certainly it is not
christian to lynch and murder them,
and yet, thoughtlessness on our part
and disinclination to seriously study
the problem really conduce to that
end. Can we not arrest the tenden-
cy in that direction? Is it not pos-
sible for Christian men and women
to do something to save the boys
and girls from perdition? At least
we can think on the subject and ser-
iously so,
But it occurs to us that we need
definite information to arouse us to
the necessity of doing more than we
are for the moral elevation of the
young and wayward. The informa-
tion can be had along many lines.
We are thinking of one special line,
and we cheerfully throw out the
hint with the hope that good may
come out of it,
Many of our readers know that
there is such a place as 'Cheltenham'
a reformatory for incorrigible col-
ored boys, and that is about all they
do know about this institution.
Many of our readers have heard
that there is such an institution in
Baltimore County, near this city,
called the Industrial Home for Col-
ored Girls, a reformatory for girls
who have gotten beyond the control
of their parents, or who have been
committed to the institutions on ac-
count of their shortcomings.
Now what we suggest is, that
some one of the largest churches in
the city hold a 'special public meet-
ing, the church crowded with all in-
terested in the improvement of our
youth along such lines and that the
superintendents of these institutions
together with as many of the Board
of Managers, of the same who may
come, to be invited to address the
colored citizens of Baltimore on the
respective institutions, giving detail-
ed information with respect to the
institutions, the inmates, their con-
dition and suggest ways of mutual
helpfulness. Mr. Dorsey, a few
months ago, appointed superinten-
dent at Cheltenham, is a most excel-
lent man aud is disposed sincerely
and honestly to do all that he can
along the line of improvement at
Cheltenham. The boys already are
drawn to him and like him very
much. Mr. Dorsey has splendid ideas,
and has had much experience; that
yet for all this he is disposed to get
a valuable hint even from a small
child.
The colored people want to know
and ought to know all about Chel-
tenham, and we feel that Mr. Dorsey
would gladly respond to such an in-
vitationand not only tell what he
knows, but kindly invite any criti-
cism or suggestion which would
have a tendency to improve condi-
tions in the interest of the boys at
the House of Reformation. By all
means let us have such a meet-
ing.
