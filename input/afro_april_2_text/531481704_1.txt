Girls Riot at
School in Md.
President of Board,
Police Restore Quiet
18 GIRLS INVOLVED
Ringleader Returned
to Home of Parents
BALTIMORE-A riot at the
Maryland Training School for
Girls at Glenburnie on Saturday
was quelled only when police and
the board president put in an ap-
pearance.
Before their arrival, however,
the girls had broken window panes
and furniture, doing damage
amounting to over $200.
Cause for the rampage, it is re-
ported, was an order that they
"turn in"-go to bed at 8 p.m.
Too Early to Go to Bed
The girls said that 8 p.m. was
too early and, since it was still
light, they would not obey. When
attempts were made to get them
to retire, some of them fled the
school grounds.
Others went to the room of
Miss Mae Rideout, the matron
who had given the order, and pro-
ceeded to damage her clothing,
the furniture, and break out win-
dow panes.
About eighteen girls were in-
volved in the disturbance, which
was finally brought under control
when nearby Ferndale police and
H. H. Murphy, president of the
board of managers, were sum-
moned.
Mr. Murphy reported that the
ringleader, a 17-year-old, who is
too old for the type of training
at the school, has been returned
to her parents.
School Problems Cited
He indicated that the school is
faced with the same problem
which obtains at Cheltenham
School for Boys: namely, that the
Institution is given all types of
girls to care for when in reality
it can only accommodate those
who are amenable to training.
He pointed out that the rioting
was led by girls with mental de-
ficiences and incorrigibles for
which it has no provision, adding
that members of his board are
meeting with the State Depart-
ment of Public Welfare with a
view towards discovering some
method of segregating them.
Extra Work" for Rioters
Restrictions and extra work
have been ordered for all the par-
ticipants in Saturday's disturbance,
Mr. Murphy declared.
Mrs. Sadye P. George, superin-
tendent of the institution, said
that the attack on Miss Rideout's
property occurred the following
morning while she (Mrs. George)
was bringing the leader of the
disturbance into town, and was
apparently an attempt to "get
even" with Miss Rideout for fore-
ing the girl to leave the school.
Mrs. George reported that most
of the damage was done in the
music room, but that the trouble
had subsided by Monday.
