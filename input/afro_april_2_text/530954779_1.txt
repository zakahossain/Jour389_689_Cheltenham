A Strange Apparition
One of the first things Mr. Wash-
ington did after starting work on
Tuskegee's grounds, was to investi-
gate a farm in Alabama where col-
ored boys were being held in semi-
slavery under white instructors, as
it is alleged is the case at Chelten-
ham.
Mr. Washington immediately
took up the matter with the Gover-
nor of Alabama and some of the
leading white citizens of the state,
and a reform school known as Mt.
Meigs was built and placed under
colored teachers. The school is:
still in existence, and colored in-
structors are on the job.

During this period, there
living in Richmond, Va., a man
whose name was Henry W. Smythe,
who was running a school for de-
linquent colored boys. Mr. Smythe
made an enviable reputation among
the white people of Virginia as a
result of his interest in colored
youth, and hence a move was start-
ed to have a colored school which
would teach the boys trades.
Baltimorean
Mr. Smythe and Booker Wash-
ington became friends. Dr. Smythe.
who was born in Baltimore, had
had tremendous influence with the
late railroad magnate, Robert Cur-
tis Ogden, who gave Ogden Hall
to Hampton Institute. Dr. Wash-
ington and Mr. Smythe induced
Mr. Ogden to come to Richmond
and meet several of the city's out-
standing white and colored men,
for the purpose of interesting Mr.
Ogden in the turning over of a
twenty-five hundred acre tract of
land which he owned, to be used
for a reformatory for colored boys.
This tract of land was situated two
and one-half miles from Hanover
in Hanover County.
Dr. Washington assured Mr.
Ogden that the land was idle and
that hundreds of colored boys, who
were getting into trouble, were
victims of circumstances, and that
a large number of such boys could
be saved if they were sent to
school and given proper training
under colored instructors. Mr. Og-
den agreed with Dr. Washington
and Mr. Smythe, so it was decided
that Mr. Ogden's attorney and an
attorney representing the state of
Virginia, draw up an agreement to
be signed by both parties.
Drew up Agreement
Th eagreement read as follows:
"I, Robert Curtis Ogden, agree to
turn over 2,500 acres of land, locat-
ed in Hanover County, which is
two and one-half miles from the
County Court House at Hanover,
Va., to be used as a reform school
for colored boys.
"Said refrom school is to be
placed under colored instructors,
and one-half of the board of gov-
ernorship must be white and the
other half colored. The state of
Virginia must pay a certain amount
pro-rata daily for each boy incar-
cerated in the school, and the Bi-
enniel Session of each legislature
must vote a certain sum of money
not to exceed two hundred thousand
dollars for new buildings and such
things which go to maintain a
school above the common prison.
"The time limit for each boy in-
carcerated must not exceed two
years unless it is found that the
boy has not improved. A school
must be maintained by the state
for the purpose of giving each boy,
who is uneducated, a fourth grade
education. Said school must be
operated under well-trained teach-
ers day and night so as to give
such boys who are working daily on
the farms of the said land to grow
food for teachers and boys, an op-
portunity to receive not less chan
a fourth grade education.
"It is further agreed that noth-
ing but colored instructors shall
ever be placed over the boys. It
is further agreed that if this con-
tract is violated, the said 2,500 acres
of land shall revert back to my es-
tate or to a contingent fund set up
by the administrators of said es-
tate."
The land was turned over to the
state, and the Virginia Manual La-
bor School started. The Virginia
Manual Labor School is still in op-
eration, and is said to be the most
oustanding reform school in the
world.
