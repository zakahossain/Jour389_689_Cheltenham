Goldsboro S. Griffith.
To the Editor of the AFRO:
No white man in Maryland
for many decades after the Civil
War labored more earnestly and
faithfully for the colored race's
betterment than did Golds-
borough S. Griffith, whose slogan
was: "It is a luxury to do good."
Mr. Griffith was born in Havre
de Grace, Md., in 1814.
It was through his efforts that
the Industrial Home for Colored
Girls, and the House of Refor-
mation for Colored Boys were
established.
The Rev. George H. Nock, in
his, "The Story of a Great Life."
says:
"Mr. Griffith's sentiment and
feeling could not be more terse-
ly expressed than in the follow-
ing words quoted from one of
his reports:
"The man who curses an-
other because he is black,
curses the God that made
his so and challenges the
infinitude of his wisdom.
"The man who says, I
hate the Negro', and calls
himself a Christian, should
read 1 John 4:20: 'If a man
say I love God, and hateth
his brother, he is a liar, for
he that loveth not his broth-
er, whom he hath seen, how
can he love God whom he
hath not seen.'
"Brethren, the time has
come when we as the strong-
er race are called upon, not
only by the demands of our
religion, but by the exigen-
cies that exist, to pray that
the Spirit of God tear away
from our hearts every ves-
tige of prejudice that hin-
ders our deep and practical
and helpful sympathy from
entering into the needs of
the colored people of this
city and state. They ask our
help they need it-they
must have it."
-
Mr. Griffith was all that the
above sentiment implies. The
writer of this article enjoyed an
intimate acquaintance and fel-
lowship with him.
Thirty-five years ago, upon the
nomination of Mr. Griffith, this
writer was elected a member of
the board of managers of the
House of Reformation, commonly
known as "Cheltenham."
(The Rev.) GEORGE F. BRAGG
827 N. Arlington Avenue.
Baltimore, Md.
