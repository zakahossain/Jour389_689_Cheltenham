His Plight at Cheltenham Is Re-
called by Inmate in '84.
To the Editor of the AFRO:
I am in favor of abolishing
Maryland's Cheitennam Reform-
atory, established in 1872. Its
motto was to reform the boys sent
there and to make them good
citizens. The institution made a
bad start, and if conditions are as
bad there now as they were in the
beginning, it should be cut out.
I was sent there from Baltimore
on August 1, 1884, when I was
11, and stayed until May 1, 1889.
I was sent for running away from
home. The guards there used a
stick or anything to beat the boys.
The average boy sent there did
not seem bad, so I feel that many
were sent to Cheltenham because
their parents did not want them.
I wrote a story to the AFRO in
1896, telling about conditions of
the place.
CHARLES E. JACKSON.
239 Norfolk Street,
Newark, N.J.
Matthews, Miss Elliot Are Called
Traitors to Ethiopia.
To the Editor of the AFRO:
The letter of Carrie Wilkins
Elliot in the July 27 issue of your
paper was evidently inspired by
the propaganda of Ralph Mat-
thews and white chiselers with
their declaration, "Ethiopians will
look down on colored Americans
as their inferiors."
She asks: "Does Ethiopia re-
gard colored Americans as kin-
dred?"
Prince Malaku Bayen, nephew
of the Ethiopian emperor, has
just returned to his royal family
with a colored American wife. A
prince with a "looked-down-
upon" wife?
Traitors and Mr. Matthews,
please explain.
This society desires to warn
traitors to Ethiopia that their
children will spit on their tomb-
stones and curse them for hav-
ing aided in selling Africa down
the gutter.
AFRICAN UNDERCOVER
DRAGON SOCIETY.
Honor Due Miss Barbour.
To the Editor of the AFRO:
The 1935 Douglass High School
year book is beautifully gotten
up, is of historical value, and re-
flects great credit on all respon-
sibile for its production.
Especially, do I want to direct
attention to the extreme appro-
priateness of its dedication. The
community can scarcely realize
its indebtedness to Miss Fannie!
L. Barbour.
At a time when the great mass-
es of our people had but faint
appreciation of education, and
the white people, generally, were
indifferent, her wonderful tri-
umph, along with that of Joseph
H. Lockerman, proving the ability
of our people to qualify as teach-
ers, paved the way for a com-
plete revolution with respect to
the educational interest of her
race.
(The Rev.)
GEORGE F. BRAGG, JR.
827 N. Arlington Avenue,

Instead of an Interracial Church.
To the Editor of the AFRO:
During the present series of
discussions concerning the or-
ganizing of interracial churches
it is specifically important that we
realize that to possess Christianity,
it is not necessary for anyone to
go to any church.
The true church so far has
never been founded, else the de-
nominational issues would not
arise.
We need no more churches, but
let those we have put Christianity
into practice.
What the masses must learn to
do is to organize militant organi-
zations which will force equality
of decent wages for work done, and
everything which pertains to the
necessities of life, including hap-
piness.
God put everything here. Man
must use his brains to obtain it
we must use the ballot.
T. E. SMITH.
513 Cherokee St.,
Bethlehem, Pa.
